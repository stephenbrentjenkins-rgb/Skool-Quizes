Step-by-step plan
1

Create your revocable living trust

The cornerstone of the whole structure. Owns your LLC, holds your home and brokerage, avoids probate on all assets — all while flowing transparently to your personal 1040.

$1,000 – $2,500
  • Hire an estate planning attorney to draft the trust document
  • You are the grantor, trustee, and beneficiary — you retain full control during your lifetime
  • Name a successor trustee (spouse, adult child, or professional) to take over at incapacity or death
  • Because it's revocable, the IRS ignores it — all income flows straight to your Form 1040
  • Avoids probate on your home, brokerage, and LLC interest — your heirs get assets without court involvement
2

Form your LLC in Texas

Direct Texas formation — no foreign registration needed. Solid charging order protection, zero state income tax, and the legal home for your business income.

~$450 setup
  • File a Certificate of Formation with the Texas Secretary of State — $300 fee
  • No foreign registration needed — you live and operate in Texas
  • Texas has no state income tax · franchise tax applies but most LLCs under ~$2.47M revenue owe $0
  • Draft an operating agreement naming your revocable trust as the sole member
  • Open a dedicated LLC bank account immediately — never mix personal and business funds
  • File annual Texas Franchise Tax Report by May 15 each year
3

Transfer assets into the structure

Each asset has its own vehicle. The home and brokerage move into the trust; the LLC captures business income; the 401(k) stays put.

$300 – $600
  • Home ($250,000) → Revocable trust via warranty deed · preserves Texas homestead exemption and property tax benefits · notify mortgage lender (Garn-St. Germain Act allows this) · update homeowner's insurance named insured
  • Brokerage ($100,000) → Trust · contact your brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, etc.) and request change of ownership · standard form, no sale, no tax event triggered
  • Business income ($75,000/yr) → LLC · all active income routes through the LLC · clients pay the LLC, not you personally · keep the LLC bank account strictly separate
  • Solo 401(k) ($50,000) → stays in your name · ERISA already protects it federally from nearly all creditors · do not transfer it into the trust
4

Open a Solo 401(k) through your LLC

The LLC unlocks the most powerful retirement account available to the self-employed. Contribute as both employee and employer for massive tax-deferred growth.

High impact
  • Employee deferral: up to $23,000/yr (2024) · $30,500 if age 50+ · pre-tax, reduces taxable income dollar-for-dollar
  • Employer contribution: up to 25% of W-2 salary (S-Corp) or ~20% of net SE income (disregarded) · paid by the LLC
  • Combined max: —/yr at your income level · $69,000 IRS cap (2024)
  • Open at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard — all offer free Solo 401(k) plans with no annual fees
  • Must be opened by December 31 of the tax year you want contributions to count
  • Employee contributions due by Dec 31 · employer contributions due by your tax filing deadline (including extensions)
5

Decide on S-Corp election

Run the breakeven math carefully. S-Corp also affects your 401(k) employer match — factor both into the decision.

Evaluate carefully
  • File Form 2553 within 75 days of LLC formation, or by March 15 for the current tax year
  • Set up payroll via Gusto or ADP (~$50/month) · run actual W-2 payroll for yourself
  • Pay yourself a reasonable salary (~$45,000) · take the remainder as distributions (no SE tax on distributions)
  • S-Corp salary is the basis for your employer 401(k) contribution — a higher salary unlocks a larger employer match
  • IRS watches S-Corp owner salaries closely — the salary must be defensible for your industry and role
6

Maintain the structure (ongoing)

Sloppy maintenance lets creditors pierce the veil. Treat the LLC as a genuinely separate legal entity every single day.

Ongoing
  • Keep LLC bank account strictly separate — document every transfer between personal and LLC accounts
  • Hold an annual LLC meeting and keep written minutes (even as sole member)
  • File Texas Franchise Tax Report annually by May 15 — most small LLCs file "No Tax Due"
  • File Form 1120-S annually if S-Corp elected · file payroll tax returns quarterly
  • Make 401(k) employee contributions by December 31 · employer contributions by your filing deadline
  • Review trust document and beneficiary designations every 2–3 years or after major life events
  • Notify your homeowner's insurance and mortgage servicer if you haven't already after the deed transfer
