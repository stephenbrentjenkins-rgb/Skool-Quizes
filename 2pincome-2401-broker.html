Texas married renters — key considerations
Renter advantage
No home means no deed transfer, no lender coordination, no homestead complexity — setup is faster and ~$300 cheaper.
Community property
All income earned during marriage is jointly owned 50/50 under Texas law — regardless of whose LLC it flows through.
Marital agreement
A partition agreement can designate future income as separate property, strengthening each LLC's creditor protection.
401(k) power
Two Solo 401(k)s = up to $138,000/yr in combined tax-deferred contributions. This is the biggest lever for renters.

Step-by-step plan
1

Create one joint revocable living trust

Both spouses are co-grantors, co-trustees, and co-beneficiaries. The trust owns both LLC interests and the brokerage — flowing transparently to your joint 1040. No home to deed makes this faster to fund.

$1,500 – $3,000
  • Both spouses named as co-trustees — either can act independently; the survivor continues without court involvement
  • Name successor trustees (adult children or professional trustee) for when both spouses are gone or incapacitated
  • The trust is assigned ownership of both LLC membership interests — it sits above both LLCs in the structure
  • Avoids probate on the brokerage and both LLC interests — critical even without real estate
  • Discuss a marital property partition agreement with your attorney to classify future LLC income as separate property, shielding each spouse's LLC from the other's creditors
  • No deed transfer needed — funding the trust is faster and ~$300 cheaper than for homeowners
2

Form two separate Texas LLCs — one per spouse

Each spouse's income flows through their own LLC, keeping liabilities ring-fenced. Two LLCs allow independent S-Corp elections and separate asset protection layers.

~$600 – $900 total
  • Spouse 1 LLC — Certificate of Formation, Texas SOS, $300 · trust is sole member · separate operating agreement
  • Spouse 2 LLC — same process, $300 · separate bank account · own operating agreement
  • Each LLC has its own dedicated bank account — never commingle between LLCs or with personal funds
  • Texas community property applies to income earned — a marital partition agreement can reclassify it as separate property
  • File annual Texas Franchise Tax Reports for each LLC by May 15 — most LLCs under ~$2.47M owe $0
  • Texas has no state income tax — LLC pass-through income is taxed federally only
3

Transfer assets into the structure

Simpler than for homeowners — no deed, no lender, no title company. Two transfers and you're done.

Quick · no deed
  • Brokerage ($150,000) → Joint trust · contact your brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, etc.) and request a change of ownership · standard form, no sale, no tax event
  • Spouse 1 income ($75,000/yr) → routes through Spouse 1 LLC · clients pay the LLC, not the individual
  • Spouse 2 income ($60,000/yr) → routes through Spouse 2 LLC · same rule applies
  • Spouse 1 401(k) ($50,000) → stays in Spouse 1's name · ERISA federal protection · do not transfer to trust
  • Spouse 2 401(k) ($30,000) → stays in Spouse 2's name · same reason
  • Each spouse must designate the other as primary 401(k) beneficiary — required by law unless waived in writing
4

Open a Solo 401(k) for each spouse

Without a home to build equity in, the Solo 401(k) is your primary wealth-building vehicle. Two spouses = two accounts = the maximum possible tax-deferred shelter.

Highest impact
  • Each spouse contributes independently: up to $23,000 employee deferral each (2024) + employer match from their LLC
  • Combined max: —/yr across both accounts at current income levels — grows entirely tax-deferred
  • Open at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard — all offer free Solo 401(k) plans with no annual fees
  • Must be opened by December 31 of the tax year you want contributions to count
  • Employee contributions due by Dec 31 · employer contributions due by your tax filing deadline including extensions
  • No Texas income tax on contributions or growth — only federal tax deferred until withdrawal
5

Evaluate S-Corp election for each LLC independently

The breakeven runs separately per spouse. One may cross the threshold while the other doesn't — that's fine and common.

Evaluate separately
  • File Form 2553 for each LLC that elects S-Corp · within 75 days of formation or by March 15 current year
  • Each S-Corp LLC files its own Form 1120-S annually and issues a K-1 to the owning spouse

6

Ongoing maintenance — both LLCs + joint trust

Two LLCs double the annual obligations. Build the routines early — sloppy maintenance is how creditors pierce the veil.

Annual obligations
  • Keep both LLC bank accounts strictly separate — no mixing between spouses' LLCs or personal accounts
  • Hold annual meetings and keep written minutes for each LLC
  • File two Texas Franchise Tax Reports by May 15 each year — one per LLC
  • File Form 1120-S for any LLC that has elected S-Corp status
  • Make 401(k) employee contributions by December 31 · employer contributions by tax filing deadline
  • Review the joint trust, wills, and both 401(k) beneficiary designations every 2–3 years
  • Revisit the marital partition agreement annually as income and assets grow
