Risk management at S&R levels — 5 questions

Question 1 of 5
For a bounce trade at support, where should your stop loss be placed?
AExactly at the support level — the moment it's breached, you're out
BJust below the support zone — giving a small buffer for normal price volatility
CAt a fixed 5% below your entry price, regardless of the level
DAt the previous swing low from several weeks ago
Correct. Placing the stop just below the zone (not right at the level) gives the trade breathing room for normal volatility — price often pokes briefly through support before bouncing. A stop right at the line can get triggered by a temporary dip that then reverses.
Your stop should go just below the support zone, not right at it. Price often briefly pokes through a level before bouncing — a stop at the exact level can get triggered unnecessarily. The small buffer separates a genuine break from normal volatility.
Question 2 of 5
Your entry is $80, stop is $76, and target is $92. What is your risk-to-reward ratio?
A1:1 — risk and reward are equal
B2:1 — reward is twice the risk
C3:1 — reward is three times the risk
D4:1 — reward is four times the risk
Correct. Risk = $80 - $76 = $4. Reward = $92 - $80 = $12. Ratio = $12 ÷ $4 = 3:1. This is an excellent setup — well above the 2:1 minimum.
Let's work it through: Risk = entry minus stop = $80 - $76 = $4. Reward = target minus entry = $92 - $80 = $12. Ratio = $12 ÷ $4 = 3:1. A strong setup.
Question 3 of 5
You have a $5,000 account and use the 2% rule. Your entry is $100 and your stop is $95. How many shares can you buy?
A10 shares
B20 shares
C50 shares
D100 shares
Correct. Max risk = 2% of $5,000 = $100. Risk per share = $100 - $95 = $5. Position size = $100 ÷ $5 = 20 shares. Total position value = $2,000 (40% of account) — but your actual risk is only $100 (2%).
Here's the calculation: Max risk = 2% × $5,000 = $100. Risk per share = entry minus stop = $100 - $95 = $5. Shares = max risk ÷ risk per share = $100 ÷ $5 = 20 shares.
Question 4 of 5
What is the purpose of a trailing stop?
ATo automatically increase your position size as profit grows
BTo move your stop in the direction of the trade as price moves in your favour, locking in profit
CTo set a wider stop so you're less likely to be stopped out
DTo automatically exit the trade at a fixed profit target
Exactly right. A trailing stop follows price as it moves in your favour, locking in gains. If the trade reverses, the trailing stop exits you — ensuring you keep most of the profit rather than giving it all back.
A trailing stop moves with price as it goes in your favour. Once price moves up (for a long trade), you move the stop up — this locks in profit and ensures you won't give it all back if the trade reverses.

AIt isn't — a perfect entry eliminates the need for risk management
BBecause even a trader who is right only 40% of the time can be profitable with a strong risk-to-reward ratio and position sizing discipline
CBecause stop losses guarantee you will never lose money
DBecause risk management replaces the need to identify support and resistance levels
Correct. With a 3:1 risk-to-reward ratio, you can be profitable with a 34% win rate. Risk management keeps you in the game through the inevitable losing trades, while a great entry strategy without it can be destroyed by one oversized loss.
Risk management matters more because it determines whether you survive losing trades — which every trader has. With proper sizing and a 3:1 ratio, you only need to be right 34% of the time to break even. Poor risk management can wipe out a good win rate in a single bad trade.
