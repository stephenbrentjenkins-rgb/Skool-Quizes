Texas community property — important for married couples
50/50 by default
All income earned during marriage is jointly owned under Texas law — regardless of whose LLC it flows through.
Creditor risk
A creditor of one spouse can potentially reach community property — making proper LLC structure even more critical.
Marital agreement
A post-nuptial partition agreement can designate income as separate property, strengthening each LLC's protection.
Joint trust
One joint revocable trust holds the home and brokerage. Each LLC membership interest is assigned to this trust.

Step-by-step plan
1

Create one joint revocable living trust

Both spouses are co-grantors, co-trustees, and co-beneficiaries. One trust owns both LLC interests, the home, and the brokerage — all still flowing to your joint 1040.

$1,500 – $3,000
  • Both spouses named as co-trustees — either can act; the survivor continues without court involvement
  • Name successor trustees (adult children, professional trustee) for when both spouses are gone or incapacitated
  • The trust assigns ownership of both LLC membership interests — it sits above both LLCs in the structure
  • Avoids probate on the home, brokerage, and LLC interests — critical in Texas where probate can be slow and expensive
  • Discuss a marital property partition agreement with your attorney to classify future income as separate property — strengthens creditor protection for each spouse's LLC
2

Form two separate Texas LLCs — one per spouse

Each spouse's income flows through their own LLC. This separates liability, preserves independent S-Corp elections, and keeps each spouse's assets ring-fenced from the other's business risks.

~$600 – $900 total
  • Spouse 1 LLC — Certificate of Formation, Texas SOS, $300 · trust is sole member · own operating agreement
  • Spouse 2 LLC — same process, $300 · separate trust assignment · own bank account
  • Each LLC has its own dedicated bank account — no commingling between the two LLCs or personal funds
  • Texas community property applies — income earned by each LLC during marriage is jointly owned; a marital partition agreement can change this
  • File annual Texas Franchise Tax Reports for each LLC by May 15 — most LLCs under ~$2.47M revenue owe $0
3

Transfer shared assets into the joint trust

The home and brokerage move into the joint trust. The 401(k)s stay in each spouse's individual name — ERISA already protects them federally.

$300 – $600
  • Home ($350,000) → Joint trust via warranty deed · preserves Texas homestead exemption (do NOT put in an LLC) · notify mortgage lender + update homeowner's insurance
  • Brokerage ($150,000) → Joint trust · request change of ownership at your brokerage · no sale, no tax event
  • Spouse 1 401(k) ($50,000) → stays in Spouse 1's name · do not move to trust · ERISA protects it
  • Spouse 2 401(k) ($30,000) → stays in Spouse 2's name · same reason
  • Each spouse must designate the other as primary 401(k) beneficiary — required by law unless waived in writing
4

Open a Solo 401(k) for each spouse

Two self-employed spouses = two Solo 401(k)s. Combined you can shelter far more income than any other retirement vehicle available.

Highest impact
  • Each spouse contributes independently: up to $23,000 employee deferral each (2024) + employer match from their respective LLC
  • Combined max: —/yr across both accounts at current income levels
  • Open at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard — free Solo 401(k) plans, no annual fees
  • Must be opened by December 31 of the tax year you want contributions to count
  • If one spouse is on the other's S-Corp payroll, they can contribute to that plan as an employee — but coordination with a CPA is required to avoid double-counting
  • Both accounts grow tax-deferred — no Texas income tax, only federal on withdrawal
5

Evaluate S-Corp election for each LLC independently

The breakeven math runs separately for each spouse. One may benefit while the other doesn't — and that's perfectly fine.

Evaluate separately
  • File Form 2553 for each LLC that elects S-Corp · within 75 days of formation or by March 15 current year
  • Each S-Corp LLC files its own Form 1120-S annually and issues a K-1 to the owning spouse

  • Spouse 2: —

6

Ongoing maintenance — both LLCs + trust

Two LLCs double the maintenance obligations. Build annual routines for both entities or the protections can unravel.

Annual obligations
  • Keep both LLC bank accounts strictly separate — no mixing between spouses' LLCs or personal accounts
  • Hold annual meetings and keep minutes for each LLC separately
  • File two Texas Franchise Tax Reports by May 15 each year
  • File Form 1120-S for each S-Corp LLC that has elected S-Corp status
  • Make 401(k) employee contributions by December 31 · employer contributions by tax filing deadline
  • Review and update the joint trust, wills, and beneficiary designations every 2–3 years or after births, deaths, divorces, or major asset changes
  • Revisit the marital partition agreement annually with your attorney as income and assets change
