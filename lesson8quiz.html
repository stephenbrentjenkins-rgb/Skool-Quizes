The S&R trading playbook — 5 questions to complete the module

Question 1 of 5
Why should you write out a trade plan before entering every trade?
AIt's a regulatory requirement for retail traders
BIt forces clarity, defines your exit points in advance, and prevents emotional decisions once the trade is live
CIt helps you remember which stock you traded
DIt's only necessary for trades over a certain size
Exactly right. A trade plan written before entry defines your rules when you're calm and objective. Once you're in a live trade, emotions can cloud judgment. The plan keeps you honest — it's your pre-agreed contract with yourself.
A trade plan prevents emotional decisions. Once you're in a live trade, the temptation to exit early, hold too long, or move your stop is strong. Writing the plan beforehand means your rules are set when you're calm — and you hold yourself to them.
Question 2 of 5
Why is paper trading recommended before using real money?
ABecause paper trading results are guaranteed to be profitable
BTo avoid paying taxes on trading profits
CIt closes the gap between knowing a concept and executing it in real time — revealing where your actual decision-making needs work
DBecause real trading platforms require a minimum of 6 months paper trading
Correct. There's a significant gap between understanding S&R theory and executing trades calmly in real time. Paper trading surfaces that gap — you'll discover your own psychological tendencies, timing issues, and decision-making weaknesses without real financial consequences.
Paper trading reveals the gap between knowing and doing. You may understand every concept in this module, but real-time execution involves psychological pressures that don't exist in theory. Paper trading lets you experience and address those pressures safely.
Question 3 of 5
What is the primary purpose of keeping a trade journal?
ATo prove to your broker that you are an active trader
BTo create a feedback loop — identifying patterns in your performance so you can improve over time
CTo track your tax obligations
DTo impress other traders in the community
Spot on. A trade journal is a feedback loop. Every trade teaches you something — but only if you capture and review it. Patterns in your journal (consistently exiting too early, taking setups that don't meet criteria) are things you can only see over a series of trades, not individual ones.
The journal is a feedback loop. By recording and reviewing your trades, you can identify patterns — good and bad — that would be invisible if you just remembered trades mentally. It's the tool that converts experience into deliberate improvement.
Question 4 of 5
You found a setup but missed the ideal entry. What should you do?
AEnter anyway at the current price — missing out is worse than a slightly worse entry
BDouble your planned position size to make up for the missed entry
CLet it go and wait for the next qualified setup — chasing trades destroys your risk-to-reward ratio
DSet a market order immediately to ensure you don't miss any more of the move
Correct. Chasing a trade after missing the entry almost always means accepting worse R:R, a wider stop, and a less favourable risk profile. The discipline to let missed trades go — and wait for the next qualified setup — is one of the most valuable habits you can build.
Let it go. Chasing trades leads to worse entries, compressed risk-to-reward ratios, and emotional decision-making. There will always be another setup. The ability to sit on your hands and wait is a genuine edge.
Question 5 of 5
What does the R multiple in your trade journal measure?
AThe total revenue generated from trading that month
BHow many times you've been right vs wrong
CThe outcome of a trade expressed in units of risk — making results comparable across different position sizes
DThe percentage return on your total account
Correct. The R multiple expresses outcomes in units of risk. A +2R trade means you made twice what you risked. A -1R trade means you lost your planned stop amount. This makes all trades comparable regardless of size — and reveals whether your setups are genuinely delivering positive expectancy over time.
R multiples measure outcomes in units of risk. If your stop is $3 per share and you make $9, that's +3R. This normalises all trades to the same scale, so a trade with 10 shares and a trade with 100 shares are directly comparable — revealing your true edge independent of position sizing.
