Step-by-step plan
1

Create one joint revocable living trust

Both spouses are co-grantors, co-trustees, and co-beneficiaries. This single trust owns both LLC interests and the brokerage — the entire structure sits beneath it.

$1,500 – $3,000
  • Both spouses named as co-trustees — either can act independently; the survivor continues without court involvement
  • Name successor trustees for when both spouses are gone or incapacitated
  • Owns both LLC membership interests — sits above both LLCs in the structure
  • Avoids probate on the brokerage and both LLC interests — keeps everything out of Texas courts
  • Ask your attorney about a marital property partition agreement to classify each spouse's LLC income as separate property, protecting it from the other's creditors
  • No home or 401(k) to fund into the trust — setup is fast and inexpensive
2

Form two separate Texas LLCs — one per spouse

Each income stream flows through its own LLC. Separate entities mean separate liability — a lawsuit against one spouse's business cannot automatically reach the other's.

~$600 – $900 total
  • Spouse 1 LLC — Certificate of Formation, Texas SOS, $300 · trust is sole member · own operating agreement
  • Spouse 2 LLC — same process, $300 · own bank account · separate operating agreement
  • Texas has no state income tax — LLC income is taxed federally only
  • File annual Texas Franchise Tax Reports for each LLC by May 15 — most LLCs under ~$2.47M revenue owe $0
  • Never commingle funds between the LLCs or personal accounts
3

Transfer assets into the structure

Just two moves: retitle the brokerage and route income through the LLCs. This is the simplest asset transfer of any version of this structure.

Quick · 2 steps
  • Brokerage ($150,000) → Joint trust · contact your brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, etc.) and request a change of ownership form · no sale, no tax event
  • Spouse 1 income ($75,000/yr) → routes through Spouse 1 LLC going forward
  • Spouse 2 income ($60,000/yr) → routes through Spouse 2 LLC going forward
  • Combined income ($135,000/yr) → routes through the shared LLC · both spouses draw from this entity
  • All clients and payers should pay the LLC directly — not the individual spouse
  • Future assets — vehicles, equipment, additional investments — can be added to the trust or LLCs as you grow
4

Evaluate S-Corp election for each LLC independently

The breakeven runs separately for each spouse. One may qualify while the other doesn't — and that's perfectly fine.

Evaluate separately
  • File Form 2553 for each LLC that elects S-Corp · within 75 days of formation or by March 15 current year
  • Each S-Corp LLC files its own Form 1120-S and issues a K-1 to the owning spouse
  • Spouse 1: —
  • Spouse 2: —
  • Set up payroll via Gusto or ADP (~$50/mo each) · pay a defensible reasonable salary · take the rest as distributions
  • Combined net savings: —
5

Ongoing maintenance — both LLCs + trust

Two LLCs double the annual obligations. Build the habits early — sloppy maintenance is how asset protection unravels.

Annual obligations
  • Keep both LLC bank accounts strictly separate from each other and from personal funds
  • Hold annual LLC meetings and keep written minutes for each entity
  • File two Texas Franchise Tax Reports by May 15 each year
  • File Form 1120-S for any LLC that has elected S-Corp status
  • Review the joint trust and beneficiary designations every 2–3 years or after major life events
  • Consider opening Solo 401(k)s for both spouses once the structure is running — your LLCs already qualify you
