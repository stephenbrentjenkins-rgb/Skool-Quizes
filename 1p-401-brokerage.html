Step-by-step plan
1

Create your revocable living trust

The foundation of your structure. Owns your LLC membership interest and holds your brokerage account — all flowing transparently to your 1040.

$1,000 – $2,500
  • Hire an estate planning attorney to draft the trust document
  • You are the grantor, trustee, and beneficiary — you lose zero control
  • Name a successor trustee (spouse, adult child, or professional)
  • Because it's revocable, all income still flows to your personal Form 1040
2

Form your LLC in Texas

Texas formation is straightforward and keeps everything in one state — no foreign registration needed. Texas offers solid charging order protection and no state income tax.

~$450 setup
  • File a Certificate of Formation with the Texas Secretary of State — $300 filing fee
  • No foreign LLC registration needed — you live and operate in Texas
  • Texas has no state income tax — only the franchise tax applies (most small LLCs owe $0)
  • Draft an operating agreement naming your revocable trust as sole member
  • Open a dedicated LLC bank account — never mix personal and business funds
  • File annual Texas Franchise Tax Report — LLCs under ~$2.47M revenue pay no tax (EZ Computation or No Tax Due)
3

Transfer assets into the structure

Move each asset into its proper vehicle. Your 401(k) is the one asset that stays outside the trust — it has its own powerful federal protection.

Simple · no deed needed
  • Brokerage ($100,000) → Contact your brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, etc.) and request a change of ownership to the trust · standard form, no sale, no tax event triggered
  • Business income ($75,000/yr) → Route all active income through the LLC · clients and payers should pay the LLC, not you personally
  • Solo 401(k) ($50,000) → Stays in your name, not the trust · already protected from creditors under federal ERISA law · no transfer needed · open one at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard as a self-employed individual
  • Any future personal property of value (vehicles, collectibles, business equipment) can be assigned to the trust or LLC as appropriate
4

Open a Solo 401(k) through your LLC

Your LLC unlocks the Solo 401(k) — the most powerful retirement account available to the self-employed. Contribute as both employer and employee for massive tax-deferred growth.

High impact
  • Employee contribution: Up to $23,000/yr (2024) · or $30,500 if you're 50+ · comes from your salary/draws · pre-tax, reducing your taxable income dollar-for-dollar
  • Employer contribution: Up to 25% of your W-2 salary (S-Corp) or ~20% of net self-employment income (disregarded entity) · paid by the LLC
  • Combined max: —/yr at your income level · grows tax-deferred until withdrawal
  • Open at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard — all offer free Solo 401(k) plans with no annual fees
  • Must be opened by December 31 of the tax year you want contributions to count
  • The 401(k) stays in your personal name — do not transfer it into the trust · ERISA already protects it from nearly all creditors
5

Decide on S-Corp election

At your income level, the math is close. Run the numbers carefully before electing.

Evaluate carefully
  • File Form 2553 within 75 days of LLC formation, or by March 15 for the current tax year
  • Set up payroll via Gusto or ADP — ~$50/month
  • Pay yourself a reasonable salary (~$45,000) · take the rest as distributions
  • Distributions are not subject to SE tax — that's where the savings come from
  • S-Corp salary also determines your employer 401(k) match — a higher salary means a larger employer contribution
6

Maintain the structure (ongoing)

Sloppy maintenance lets creditors pierce the veil. Treat the LLC as a real, separate entity.

~$2,500–$3,000/yr
  • Keep LLC bank account strictly separate — document every transfer
  • Hold an annual LLC meeting and keep minutes (even as sole member)
  • File Texas Franchise Tax Report annually by May 15 — most small LLCs owe $0
  • File Form 1120-S annually if S-Corp elected
  • Make 401(k) contributions by December 31 each year (employee) and by your tax filing deadline (employer)
  • Review trust and beneficiary designations every 2–3 years
