Trading S&R levels — bounces & breakouts — 5 questions

Question 1 of 5
In a bounce trade, what is your confirmation signal before entering?
APrice has been at the support level for three or more days
BA rejection candle — typically with a long wick into the level but a close back on the correct side
CThe stock appears on a trending news feed
DVolume drops significantly as price approaches the level
Correct. The rejection candle — especially one with a long wick — shows that price tested the level and was rejected. It's evidence that buyers (at support) or sellers (at resistance) are defending the level. That's your entry signal.
The confirmation you're looking for is a rejection candle — ideally with a long wick into the level and a close back on the correct side. This shows the level is holding and traders are defending it.
Question 2 of 5
Where should your stop loss be placed on a bounce trade from support?
AAt the exact support level price bounced from
BAt the previous resistance level above
CJust below the support zone — if price breaks convincingly through, the bounce has failed
DAt a fixed 5% below your entry price
Exactly right. Placing your stop just below the support zone means: if price breaks convincingly through your support level, you're out immediately. The level has failed. A small planned loss beats a large unplanned one.
Your stop goes just below the support zone. If price breaks convincingly below support, the setup has failed and you want to be out. Placing it exactly at the level risks getting stopped out by normal price volatility within the zone.
Question 3 of 5
What is the key difference between a genuine breakout and a fakeout?
AGenuine breakouts always happen on Mondays; fakeouts happen later in the week
BA genuine breakout has a full candle body closing beyond the level, often on elevated volume. A fakeout only has a wick through the level with the body closing back inside.
CFakeouts only happen on small-cap stocks; genuine breakouts only happen on large-caps
DA genuine breakout means price never returns to the level again
Spot on. The body of the candle tells the real story. A wick through a level with the body closing back inside means sellers (at resistance) or buyers (at support) ultimately won that period — it's a fakeout signal. A full body close beyond the level is genuine momentum.
The key is where the candle BODY closes, not just where the wick reaches. A wick through the level with a body close back inside = fakeout. A full candle body closing beyond the level, ideally on elevated volume = genuine breakout.
Question 4 of 5
You're considering a breakout trade. The candle has just closed above resistance. When do you enter?
AThe moment price crosses above the resistance line mid-candle
BAfter waiting three days to confirm the breakout is real
CAt the open of the next candle after the breakout candle has closed
DOnly after price has pulled back and retested the broken resistance level
Correct. Waiting for the candle to close gives you confirmation before committing. Entering mid-candle is risky — the candle could reverse and close back below the level (a fakeout). The open of the next candle is your entry.
Never enter mid-candle on a breakout. Wait for the breakout candle to fully close beyond the level, then enter at the open of the following candle. This simple rule significantly reduces your exposure to fakeouts.
Question 5 of 5
Your entry is at $50, your stop is at $48, and your target is at $54. Should you take this trade?
AYes — any trade with a profit target is worth taking
BNo — the risk-to-reward ratio is only 2:1, which is the minimum but acceptable
CYes — the risk is $2 and the reward is $4, giving a 2:1 ratio which meets the minimum threshold
DNo — you need at least a 5:1 ratio to take any trade
Correct. Risk = $2 (entry $50 minus stop $48), Reward = $4 (target $54 minus entry $50). That's a 2:1 ratio — exactly the minimum. This trade meets the threshold. A higher ratio would be better, but 2:1 is acceptable.
Let's work it out: risk = $50 - $48 = $2. Reward = $54 - $50 = $4. That gives a 2:1 reward-to-risk ratio — the minimum threshold. The trade is acceptable. Any ratio below 2:1 and you should pass on the setup.
