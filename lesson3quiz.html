Lesson 3 Knowledge Check

Identifying support & resistance — 5 questions

Question 1 of 5
Why should you zoom out to 6–12 months before drawing S&R levels?
ASo the chart loads faster on TradingView
BBecause levels built up over a longer period are stronger and more significant
CSo you can see more candles and find more levels to draw
DBecause short-term charts are only for professional traders
Correct. A level that's been respected five times over a year carries far more weight than one that appeared twice last week. The history tells you how many traders are aware of — and responding to — that level.
The reason is about strength, not convenience. Levels tested over a longer timeframe have been seen by more traders — making them more significant and more likely to hold.
Question 2 of 5
When identifying S&R levels, why do we "look left" on the chart?
ABecause the most recent price action is always on the left
BTo find where price has previously stalled or reversed — that history tells us where levels are likely to form again
CBecause left-side levels are always stronger than right-side levels
DTo see what the stock was priced at when it first launched
Exactly right. Price has memory. Where it has reversed before, it's likely to react again — because the same groups of traders are watching those same levels. The left side of the chart is your evidence.
Looking left means studying past price behaviour. The evidence for where levels exist is always to the left — that's where the reversals, bounces, and stalls have already happened.
Question 3 of 5
Why should you draw support and resistance as a zone rather than an exact price line?
ABecause TradingView doesn't support exact price lines
BTo make the chart look less cluttered
CBecause price rarely bounces at the exact same number each time — it reacts within a range
DBecause zones are easier to draw than exact lines
Spot on. Price doesn't bounce at $155.00 to the penny every time. It reacts in a zone — sometimes $154.20, sometimes $156.80. Drawing a zone accounts for this and prevents you from being stopped out unnecessarily.
Price behaviour isn't precise — it reacts within a range, not at an exact number. Drawing a zone rather than a line accounts for this natural variability and keeps you flexible.
Question 4 of 5
A support level has been tested and held four times over the past eight months. How would you rate this level?
AWeak — price keeps returning to it, which means it will eventually break
BIrrelevant — old levels don't matter once enough time has passed
CStrong — four touches over eight months means many traders are watching and defending that level
DModerate — you need at least ten touches for a level to be truly strong
Correct. Four touches = a very strong level. Every touch adds more evidence and more trader awareness. More traders watching a level means a more significant reaction when price approaches it again.
More touches = stronger level, not weaker. Four touches over eight months means a large number of traders have seen price react there — making them more likely to act on it in the future.
Question 5 of 5
You're analysing a chart and can identify eight possible S&R levels. What should you do?
ADraw all eight — more levels give you more trading opportunities
BNarrow it down to the two or three most obvious and well-tested levels only
CPick the highest and lowest levels and ignore everything in between
DOnly use levels from the last 30 days
Exactly right. Less is more. Two or three strong, obvious levels will serve you far better than eight questionable ones. If a level doesn't jump out at you immediately, it probably isn't strong enough to trade from with confidence.
More levels = more confusion, not more opportunity. The goal is to identify the two or three that are most obvious and well-tested. Quality over quantity — every time.
